# 2026-05

## PyPI Safety & Security Engineer (Mike Fiedler)

### Malware Response

June's malware work was dominated by an active, coordinated campaign
that published malicious packages across many accounts and projects.
Mike worked through it in batches over the second half of the month,
prioritizing removal of the malicious releases,
[advisories](https://github.com/pypa/advisory-database/pull/329) for the confirmed-malicious packages,
and unblocking of the legitimate maintainers caught up in it.
Auto-quarantine contained most of the user-facing exposure automatically,
blocking downloads ahead of manual review while Mike worked the longer tail of removals and follow-up.

Separately, Mike acted on a [report of leaked PyPI credentials from researchers at GitGuardian](https://blog.gitguardian.com/hunting-leaked-pypi-tokens-62-live-125-packages-exposed/),
confirming which exposed tokens were still valid, invalidating the live ones,
and notifying the affected account holders on PyPI.
This is the same long-lived-credential exposure that Trusted Publishing is designed to remove.

A 30-day snapshot shows the corroboration model working as intended.
Of 356 reports across 252 packages, the 182 corroborated by two or more
independent observers were 100% accurate, against about 83% for single-observer reports.
That is what lets corroborated reports trigger action automatically,
and roughly 77% of reported packages were auto-quarantined ahead of any manual review.

Mike also improved the tooling around malware response:
extending [observer statistics to cover releases as well as projects](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/20225)
now that quarantine can act at the release level,
adding admin actions to [close sibling reports at once](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/20212)
and [notify users when an API token is removed](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/20133),
fixing two release-deletion admin bugs ([scope selector](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/20211),
[multiple-versions POST](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/20222)),
and adding a [trigger that clears orphaned release descriptions](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/20216).
He verified the [`pull_request_target` restriction](https://github.com/pypi/warehouse/pull/19601)
for GitHub Trusted Publishers is enforced at upload.
Beyond the campaign advisories he published one for [`durabletask`](https://github.com/pypa/advisory-database/pull/310)
and kept the advisory database current.

## Security Developer-in-Residence (Seth Larson)

### Mitigating API authentication bypass for "python.org"

Back in February 2026 the PSRT received a report for an
authentication bypass vulnerability in "python.org" that
had existed in the codebase for 14 years undetected. The
report was triaged, confirmed, and mitigated in less than
48 hours. What happened after to ensure the safety of Python
users in the case this wasn't the only vulnerability
was only possible due to Alpha-Omega funding:

* Audit of database backups and logs for python.org to show
  this vulnerability had not been exploited.
* Additional mitigations and hardening applied to the codebase
  including requiring URLs to begin with "https://www.python.org/"
  for metadata/verification materials.
* Follow-up verification of all materials available on python.org/downloads (GPG and Sigstore)
* Solicitation and completion of a third-party audit from Trail of Bits
  through the "Patch the Planet" program. Audit was completed and findings
  remediated for python.org and python/release-tools repository.
* Publication of the vulnerability and work with timelines.

The full report is available here: https://blog.python.org/2026/06/mitigated-api-bypass-for-download-metadata-python-dot-org/
