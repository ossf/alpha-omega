## AI Security Engineers in Residence

In June, the team built out the foundational infrastructure needed to run the AI Security Engineers in Residence program at scale. Dushan led the technical buildout, enhancing the Scrutineer platform with multiple PRs for the team’s needs, standing up the services for managing findings and maintainer outreach, loading the first tranche of gems for scanning, and fully documenting the dev workflow and findings-sharing process. Considerable time across the team also went into account administration: securing Anthropic/Claude access for all SEiRs due to some friction, and onboarding Kou from Ruby Core.

With infrastructure largely in place, the team shifted into active scanning and policy work. Colby completed first-pass scans of actionpack and railties (no findings) and nokogiri (2 low-severity findings, still pending validation). Matt ran exploratory scans across several gems (text, discovery, warden, bundler, action\_view) and made progress on the Proof of Concept, Validation, and Exploit-Handling Policy. Patrick drafted the maintainer outreach policy and began work on a coordinated vulnerability disclosure (CVD) policy, alongside compiling the first group of trusted publishing projects. Kou, collaborating from the Ruby Core side, tested Scrutineer against CRuby and used Claude Code to review io\_buffer.c, surfacing several SEGV cases — one of which has already been reviewed and fixed via a merged PR.

On the governance and coordination side, Dushan drafted a policy document on community governance and the Vulnerability Response Lead role, and the team began cross-ecosystem coordination with peers in other language communities. Marty's involvement centered on program-level support: managing requests from outside companies and maintainers wanting to join the effort (while deliberately keeping the core team lean for now), staying connected to Alpha-Omega's weekly SEiR sync, and sharing feedback on the effectiveness of the weekly sync with Mirko. 

Finally, the team drafted the [initial SEiR program page](https://rubygems.org/pages/security-engineers-in-residence-faq) on RubyGems.org to provide a FAQ for the Ruby community with clear guidance on how the team should be contacted.

Looking forward, Colby will finalize the SEiR workshop content, to be given at RubyConf on July 15th. With the infrastructure in place, the team plans to ramp up the number of gems being scanned and remediated.

### Glasswing Program Metrics — June 16–30, 2026

This marks the first reporting period for the Glasswing security program's metrics dashboard, establishing a baseline against which future progress will be measured. During this two-week window, the team identified 5 vulnerabilities across the gems under review, with 4 confirmed or reproduced — indicating a strong validation rate against initial findings. Of the identified vulnerabilities, the severity breakdown skewed toward lower-impact issues: 1 medium-severity finding and the remainder presumably low or unclassified, with zero critical or high-severity vulnerabilities surfaced in this window.

The program is actively supporting 16 projects, with the majority of activity sourced from the team's own proactive scanning efforts (17 scanning events) rather than inbound community reports (1). This reflects the SEiR team's emphasis on systematic, proactive security review over reactive triage in this early phase. Coverage currently stands at 38%, giving a clear starting benchmark for how much of the target gem ecosystem has been assessed so far.

On the response side, no maintainer outreaches or fixes were recorded yet in this period, and no embargo breaches or rapid-response events occurred — consistent with a program still in its scanning and validation phase rather than remediation. Fix rate and acknowledgment rate are not yet calculable given no fixes or outreaches have been logged. The team's work has been tracked via the Glasswing project board.

Because this is the baseline period, all "(new)" columns are unset by design — every metric above represents the program's starting point. Going forward, each reporting period will show incremental ("new") activity layered against these totals, giving Alpha-Omega a clearer week-over-week view of program momentum, including time-to-report and time-to-fix medians once remediation activity begins.
